About

Research profile

I am a PhD student in Computer Science at the University of Hawai‘i at Mānoa. My research focuses on long-context large language models, with particular interests in memory mechanisms, efficient inference, and scalable reasoning systems. I investigate how large models store, retrieve, and utilize information under practical constraints, and how these processes can be improved through system-level and algorithmic innovations.

My academic background spans mathematics, scientific computing, and education. I completed an MSc in Scientific and Data Intensive Computing at University College London (UCL), followed by an MA in Education Science, where I developed a strong interest in learning processes, knowledge representation, and instructional design. This interdisciplinary background informs my current research, especially in understanding how artificial systems can model and support complex reasoning.

Research

Selected projects

Project 01

Scientific Reproducibility Assessment with Large Language Models

Investigating the ability of large language models to evaluate and reproduce scientific content. This work focuses on assessing methodological understanding, factual consistency, and reasoning fidelity in AI-generated scientific outputs using automated metrics and structured evaluation pipelines.

Project 02

Efficient Long-Context LLM Inference

Studying scalable attention and KV-cache optimization techniques for long-context large language models. This work explores ANN-based attention, memory compression, and GPU-efficient retrieval mechanisms to improve inference efficiency.

Project 03

Memory Mechanisms in Large Language Models

Examining how large language models represent and retrieve information across extended contexts. The project investigates the limitations of attention-based memory and explores structured or hybrid memory architectures.

Project 04

Reliability in Multi-Step Reasoning

Investigating how large language models behave in extended reasoning processes, with a focus on the stability and reliability of intermediate steps. This work explores how reasoning quality evolves across steps and how it can be characterized through model-intrinsic signals.
